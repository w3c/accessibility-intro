The power of the Web is in its universality.
Access by everyone regardless of disability is an essential aspect.
Tim Berners-Lee, W3C Director and inventor of the World Wide Web

The Web is designed to work for all people, whatever their technology, language, location, or ability. When the Web meets this goal, it is accessible to people with a diverse range of hearing, movement, sight, and cognitive ability.

This radically changes the impact of disability, because the Web removes barriers to communication that many people face in the physical world. However, when websites, technologies, or tools are badly designed, they can create barriers that exclude people from using the Web.

The mission of the Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) is to lead the Web to its full potential as an accessible platform, enabling people with disabilities to participate equally.

Why: The Case for Web Accessibility

The Web must be accessible to provide equal access and equal opportunity for people with abilities. The UN Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities recognizes access to information and communications technologies, including the Web, as a basic human right.

Accessibility supports social inclusion for people with disabilities as well as others, like older people, or people in rural areas and developing countries.  Accessibility also benefits people without disabilities. The Web Accessibility Perspectives videos show how accessibility is essential for people with disabilities while useful for everyone.

There is also a strong business case for accessibility. Case studies show that accessible websites enjoy many benefits including better search results, reduced maintenance costs, and increased audience reach. Developing a Web Accessibility Business Case for Your Organization details the benefits of web accessibility.

What: Examples of Web Accessibility

Properly designed websites and tools can be used by people with disabilities. However, currently most sites and tools are developed with accessibility barriers that make it difficult and often impossible for some people to use them. Below are just a few examples.

Alternative Text for Images

Images should include equivalent alternative text in the markup/code. If alt text isn't provided for images, people who cannot see and use a screen reader (that reads aloud the information on a page), will only hear “image” or perhaps the file name.  They will not receive the information presented by the image.

When equivalent alt text is provided, the information is available to people who are blind, as well as to those who turn off images (perhaps to conserve bandwidth for a rural connection or mobile device). It's also made available to technologies that cannot see images, such as search engines.

Keyboard Input

Some people cannot use a mouse as it requires seeing the cursor and fine motor controls to use it.  An accessible website does not rely on the mouse; it provides all functionality via the keyboard. This includes things like “mouse-over” popups and drag-and-drop functionality.

 

Transcripts for Audio

Just as images aren't available to people who can't see, audio files aren't available to people who can't hear. Providing a text transcript makes the audio information accessible to people who are deaf or hard of hearing, as well as to search engines and other technologies that can't hear.

How: Make Your Website and Web Tools Accessible

Most of the basics of accessibility are fairly easy to implement, however, if you are new to accessibility, it takes some time and effort to learn the common issues and solutions. Here are places to start:

  • Accessibility Principles - an introduction to accessibility requirements and standards.
  • Intro to Accessibility Evaluation - an introduction to testing the accessibility of a web page.
  • Tips for Getting Started - basic considerations for designing, writing, and developing for web accessibility.
  • Understanding WCAG 2.0 – detailed and in-depth information.

Accessibility is essential for developers and organizations that do not want to exclude people from using their products and services.

Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) at W3C

The W3C Web Accessibility Initiative (WAI) brings together people from industry, disability organizations, government, and research labs around the world to develop guidelines and resources to help make the Web accessible.

WAI's scope includes a variety of items including:

  • web content (websites and web applications)
  • authoring tools (such as content management systems (CMS) and blogging software)
  • browsers and other “user agents”
  • and W3C technical specifications, including WAI-ARIA for accessible rich Internet applications.

We invite individuals and organizations to participate in WAI by reviewing guidelines, submitting supporting documentation, contributing to the WAI Interest Group, or participating in Working Groups.
